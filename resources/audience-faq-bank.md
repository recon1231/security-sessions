# Audience FAQ Bank

Common questions that come up in beginner/general security sessions, with prepared, plain-language answers. Useful for staying consistent across multiple sessions and for handling Q&A confidently.

---

**"Is antivirus enough to keep me safe?"**
No — antivirus mainly catches known malware signatures. It doesn't stop phishing, social engineering, or attacks that trick *you* into handing over access voluntarily. Think of it as one layer, not a full solution.

**"I have two-factor authentication, so I'm safe from phishing, right?"**
Traditional MFA (SMS codes, app-based codes) helps a lot, but modern Adversary-in-the-Middle phishing kits can steal your active session after you've already passed MFA. Hardware security keys (FIDO2/WebAuthn) are currently the strongest defense against this specific attack.

**"How do I know if a link is actually safe?"**
Hover over it (on desktop) to see the real destination before clicking. On mobile, when in doubt, don't click — go to the site directly by typing the address or using a bookmark instead.

**"Is it illegal to look someone up using OSINT?"**
Searching publicly available information is generally legal — that's the definition of "open source." It becomes a problem the moment you access private/non-public data without authorization, or use the information to harass, stalk, or harm someone. Legal doesn't automatically mean ethical, either — always consider intent.

**"Can hackers really find my location from a photo?"**
Not usually from metadata anymore (most platforms strip it automatically), but visual details in the photo itself — signs, landmarks, reflections — can still narrow down a location. Worth a second look before posting.

**"What's the single most important thing I can do to be safer online?"**
Unique passwords for every account, stored in a password manager, plus a hardware security key or authenticator app instead of SMS for your most important accounts. This alone eliminates most of the common attack paths.

**"Should I be worried about the dark web?"**
Less than headlines suggest. Most "dark web" exposure for individuals comes from old data breaches your email was already part of, not something uniquely dangerous. Checking a legitimate breach-notification service periodically is more useful than panic.
